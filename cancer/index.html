Ch. 01 — The bottom line

Ch. 02 — The whole story

Five years, on one line.

Four separate primaries — all on the left side — surfaced one after another between late 2021 and mid-2023. Then a recurrence appeared on the opposite (right) side of the neck. That right-sided node carried extranodal extension, which is the single most important pathological detail in this entire file: it is the reason chemoradiation followed surgery. Since chemoradiation, all imaging has been clean.

Surgery / primary tumor Recurrence with ENE Chemoradiation Clean scan / NED

Why four primaries before age 62?

The background that made the oral cavity especially susceptible is oral lichen planus, a chronic immune-mediated inflammation present since 2017. Long-standing erosive OLP carries a known but small risk of malignant transformation, and once one cancer arises, the same field tends to produce more — a phenomenon called field cancerization. Each of the four primaries was small and early-stage, all caught at pT1.

Three were on the left side of the mouth (lateral tongue, maxillary ridge, ventral tongue). The fourth event was different — it was the right-sided neck node, far from any visible primary, behaving as a regional recurrence.

The pivot point: extranodal extension.

Of all the pathology details in this file, the most decisive is the 12/19/2023 right neck dissection finding: one 1.3 cm lymph node with extranodal extension (ENE). ENE means tumor cells had spread beyond the capsule of the node — the strongest indication that chemoradiation is necessary after surgery, per the joint analysis of RTOG-9501 and EORTC-22931.

Without ENE, observation might have been considered. With ENE, the NCCN and both consulting institutions (Kaiser and Cedars-Sinai) recommended adjuvant cisplatin-IMRT to the oral cavity and bilateral neck. That recommendation was followed in early 2024.

Ch. 03 — The tumor's DNA

A molecular portrait.

In January 2024, Caris Life Sciences ran a 700+ gene profile on the right-neck node specimen. Two findings are worth carrying forward: PD-L1 positive (CPS 5) — meaning immune-checkpoint drugs like pembrolizumab would be a real option if anything ever came back — and a PIK3CA E542K hotspot mutation, which opens the door to PI3K-inhibitor clinical trials. Equally important are the things not there: no HPV, no EBV, low mutational burden, microsatellite-stable.

The germline question, in plain language.

Tumor sequencing can't always tell what's inherited from what's acquired by the tumor. Two of the most important mutations here — TP53 R213P and CDKN2A R80* — show up at fractions (34% and 36%) consistent with being inherited rather than tumor-only.

Combine that with four primary cancers before age 62 and a mother with melanoma + uterine cancer, and the case for formal germline testing — via a cancer genetics counselor, ideally on a non-blood specimen like cultured skin fibroblasts — is strong.

If germline TP53 is confirmed — the Toronto Protocol.

Li-Fraumeni syndrome has a published screening protocol shown to detect tumors earlier and improve survival: annual whole-body MRI, annual brain MRI, breast MRI (mammogram avoided), abdominal ultrasound every 3–4 months, comprehensive labs.

If germline CDKN2A is confirmed instead (or as well), the CAPS Consortium protocol adds annual pancreatic surveillance with alternating EUS and MRI/MRCP plus annual full-skin dermatology. Daughters and other first-degree relatives may also want testing.

The cholesterol story.

LDL of 204 mg/dL is high enough that the PCP, Dr. Kavafyan, has already written back: "I highly recommend restarting the cholesterol medication I prescribed at least 3 times per week." The previous reading in January 2026 was 177; this is trending the wrong direction.

Beyond the cardiovascular benefit, there are observational data linking statin use to improved survival in HNSCC and to better response to checkpoint inhibitors. The cancer-side argument and the heart-side argument point the same direction. Worth a conversation about resuming the prescription daily rather than three times a week, and pairing it with a Mediterranean-style diet pattern.

The low blood count story.

A WBC of 3.0 and lymphocyte count of 591 are mildly low. After cisplatin chemoradiation, mild persistent leukopenia and lymphopenia are common and often don't require intervention — bone marrow can take years to fully recover.

What's worth tracking: the trend over the next 6–12 months (rising, stable, or falling), and whether either drops further. Given the TP53 mutation profile and prior platinum exposure, a once-yearly CBC with differential is a reasonable safety net for the very small risk of treatment-related myelodysplasia. Not a cause for alarm — just for vigilance.

Ch. 05 — The action plan

What to do now.

A clean scan is not a finish line — it's a foothold. There is no active cancer to "cure" today; the work is to protect this remission. The highest-leverage actions fall into four buckets: genetics, oral & dental, lifestyle, and surveillance. Below is a prioritized list to bring to the care team — each item with a specific ask to make the conversation concrete.

01
Get formal germline genetic testing

Two of the strongest mutations in the Caris report — TP53 and CDKN2A — sit at fractions suggesting they could be inherited. Add four primary cancers in one decade and a mother with melanoma + uterine cancer. This is the single highest-value next step, both for understanding the original disease and for the daughter and family.

Ask: referral to UCLA or City of Hope cancer genetics for multi-gene germline panel, ideally on skin fibroblasts.
02
Lock in the dental + ORN-prevention plan

Bilateral neck radiation puts jaw bone at risk for osteoradionecrosis — a feared late complication, especially around any future dental extraction. The PENTO protocol (pentoxifylline + vitamin E) is now widely used both to treat early ORN and prophylactically before extractions in irradiated bone.

Ask: post-radiation dental check every 3–6 months · custom nightly fluoride tray · PENTO protocol primed for any future extraction.
03
Treat the cholesterol — daily, not 3× weekly

LDL 204 is well above goal and trending up. Statins are well-tolerated, cardioprotective, and — separately — associated with improved survival in HNSCC in observational data. The PCP's note is the green light. Pair with diet pattern: more plants, less saturated fat, less sugar.

Ask: resume statin daily; recheck lipids in 8–12 weeks; consider a registered dietitian referral.
04
Continue tight oral medicine surveillance for lichen planus

The oral lichen planus didn't go away when the cancers were removed. With four primaries in this field and an HPV-negative SCC background, surveillance for new dysplastic lesions must remain lifelong. Topical clobetasol has data suggesting it actually delays malignant transformation.

Ask: oral medicine specialist every 3 months · biopsy any new or changing lesion promptly · continue topical steroid as advised.
05
Check vitamin D — and supplement if low

A meta-analysis across 16 studies (n > 81,000) linked higher vitamin D status to lower H&N cancer incidence and improved survival. Vitamin D also reduces radiation-related mucositis. Simple, cheap, low risk.

Ask: order 25-OH-Vitamin-D level; aim for ≥ 30 ng/mL with cholecalciferol if low.
06
Lock in the surveillance schedule

NCCN's standard for resected HNSCC: H&P every 2–6 months in year 2, every 4–8 months years 3–5, annually after that — plus annual TSH. After a clean post-treatment PET-CT, routine PET-CT isn't required in an asymptomatic patient. CT becomes the default.

Ask Dr. Scher to put the next 24 months of imaging and clinic visits on the calendar in advance.
07
Address late-effect rehab proactively

Two years out from bilateral neck IMRT, late effects creep in slowly: neck stiffness/fibrosis, swallowing changes, xerostomia, lymphedema. Active rehab beats reactive rehab — and the voice matters professionally.

Ask: head & neck speech-language pathology evaluation · CLT-LANA lymphedema therapist · pilocarpine or cevimeline trial if dry mouth is bothersome.
08
Movement, strength, sleep — the unglamorous things

ACSM survivorship guidelines: 150 min/week moderate aerobic + 2× resistance training. Resistance is especially important after CRT because muscle mass tends to drop quietly (sarcopenia). At 117 lb / 5'3", maintaining lean mass matters. Mediterranean dietary pattern (PREDIMED-style) has the best evidence for cancer-survivor outcomes.

Ask: survivorship-oriented physical therapist or oncology trainer who knows post-CRT bodies.
