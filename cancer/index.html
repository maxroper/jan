What "moderate dysplasia" means

Oral epithelial dysplasia is a spectrum: mild → moderate → severe → carcinoma in situ → invasive SCC. Moderate is the middle of that range and is treated as high-grade under the WHO binary system. Reported rates of malignant transformation vary widely with patient context: low-risk patients see ~10% transformation over years; in a patient with prior multifocal SCC + lichen planus background + post-radiation field, the figure is substantially higher.

Typical management in this setting

In a high-risk patient the standard approach is surgical excision with negative margins, often as a small in-office or outpatient procedure. Close follow-up replaces routine surveillance. The presence of generalized periodontitis means infection control / oral hygiene optimization runs in parallel — and the lesion's location in a previously irradiated field requires the surgeon to factor osteoradionecrosis risk (PENTO consideration) into the plan.

Deep-dive · what the latest literature says about this biopsy

Ch. 02b · evidence digest, 2024–2026

A focused literature scan changes the framing in three important ways. (1) The pattern of four primaries + lichen planus + new bilateral gingival changes likely meets criteria for a specific entity called PMOL, not just "dysplasia on lichen planus background." (2) The 2024 American Head and Neck Society guideline is now the most authoritative US document on management of this exact problem. (3) Two investigational treatments — topical imiquimod and intralesional nivolumab — are emerging with promising data that may matter to her specifically.

Does Jan have PMOL?

The 2021 Woo/Thompson expert consensus on PVL/PMOL requires: (a) multifocal disease (≥2 separate sites), (b) progression over time (new sites, expansion, or histologic worsening), and (c) exclusion of clear etiology like heavy tobacco use. Jan's profile — never-smoker female, four prior primaries 2021–2023, oral lichen planus background, now new bilateral gingival lesions (one moderate dysplasia, one hyperkeratosis) — fits the demographic and progression criteria well.

Pooled malignant transformation rate for PMOL: 43–74% over follow-up, vs ~10% for ordinary moderate dysplasia and ~1.4% for OLP alone. If accepted as a PMOL diagnosis, surveillance intensity escalates — quarterly oral medicine review with photographic field mapping, lower threshold for biopsy of any new lesion, and multidisciplinary co-management.

What AHNS 2024 actually recommends

The American Head and Neck Society's Cancer Prevention Service published an updated 2024 guideline (Gates et al., Head & Neck, Nov 2024 — the most current US expert consensus on this exact problem).

  • Formal excision or ablation for any dysplastic lesion (mild, moderate, or severe) when feasible.
  • Excisional margins: 2–5 mm in depth and width.
  • Clinical surveillance every 3 months for moderate dysplasia.
  • Re-biopsy every 12–18 months for moderate dysplasia even if visually stable.
  • Active surveillance for at least 5 years — transformation risk persists 10–15 years.
  • No single chemopreventive agent has durable efficacy as monotherapy.
Molecular workup worth requesting on the excised tissue
  • p53 IHC pattern — distinguishes mutant pattern (overexpression/null) from wild-type. A mutant pattern argues for true neoplastic clonal expansion rather than reactive inflammation.
  • p16 IHC — given her CDKN2A R80* mutation, expect loss; confirms HPV-independent pathway.
  • Ki-67 proliferation index — high suprabasal staining predicts higher transformation risk.
  • LOH at 3p14, 9p21, 17p13 — the classical Rosin/Zhang field-cancerization panel.
  • Repeat NGS on the new lesion — does it share the same TP53 R213P, CDKN2A R80*, PIK3CA E542K signature as her 2023 tumor? Shared mutations → clonal field expansion. Different mutations → independent new primary. This changes prognostic framing.
  • Tumor-informed ctDNA (Signatera / NeXT Personal / Haystack MRD) — personalized panel built from her 2023 tumor for blood-based surveillance.
Investigational chemoprevention worth knowing about
  • Metformin for oral premalignancy — Phase IIa (Gutkind 2021) positive; Phase IIb NCT05237960 ongoing. Generally well-tolerated.
  • Topical imiquimod 5% — 2025 two-center retrospective: 65–82% had ≥50% size reduction; 32–42% complete resolution. Trial NCT07210775 active.
  • Intralesional nivolumab — Phase II NCT06561087 in high-risk OPL. Early data: 85% clinical / 41% histologic improvement at low doses. Her CPS 5 makes her tumor biology a biomarker match.
  • Curcumin 3.6 g/day — Phase IIB Kuriakose 2016: 67.5% vs 55.3% response. Safe adjunct.
  • Photodynamic therapy (5-ALA) — 86% overall response in oral leukoplakia in published cohort; useful where surgery is morbid.
  • 13-cis-retinoic acid · erlotinib (EPOC) — both negative in long-term trials. Not standard.
Pragmatic note
None of the above is FDA-approved for oral premalignancy as monotherapy. Surgical excision remains primary; these are adjuncts or trial options for diffuse / recurrent disease.
Periodontitis as an active driver

Dr. Kang's clinical note describes "generalized periodontitis and gingival inflammation" as the context for the biopsy. Recent literature (2023–2025) increasingly identifies F. nucleatum and P. gingivalis — the two main periodontitis bacteria — as active drivers of oral SCC progression, not just passengers. A 2025 study in Infectious Agents and Cancer showed their co-infection accelerates the cell cycle and amplifies pro-inflammatory pathways in oral cancer cells.

Two practical implications: (1) Active gingival inflammation can mimic dysplasia histologically — controlling the periodontitis aggressively before any re-biopsy or excision improves diagnostic clarity. (2) Aggressive periodontal management may itself be cancer-prevention, not just dental hygiene.

Operating in a previously radiated field

The right posterior maxillary alveolus sits within her 2024 right neck IMRT field. Osteoradionecrosis (ORN) is a real, if low-probability, complication of any procedure in that field.

  • PENTO protocol (pentoxifylline 400 mg BID + vitamin E 1000 IU daily) starting 1 wk pre-procedure, continued 8 wks post — supported by 2023–2025 reviews.
  • Hyperbaric oxygen no longer first-line after the 2019 HOPON trial showed no benefit for prophylaxis.
  • Maxillary ORN rarer than mandibular (better blood supply) — but post-IMRT maxilla is not no-risk.
  • Atraumatic technique, primary closure where possible, antibiotic prophylaxis (amoxicillin or clindamycin if penicillin-allergic).

Lichen planus treatment in a cancer survivor

A 2024 retrospective in BDJ Open compared OLP treatments and found topical clobetasol associated with the lowest subsequent OSCC risk (HR 0.73) among studied OLP therapies. Tacrolimus showed a weaker protective signal and carries theoretical (though clinically weak) concerns about long-term local immunosuppression in cancer survivors. If pharmacotherapy is needed, clobetasol is preferred over tacrolimus in this setting.

Pattern 01 · most consequential if confirmed
Confidence: moderate–strong · pending germline test

An inherited DNA-repair vulnerability may be the underlying engine

Her tumor showed TP53 R213P at 34% VAF and CDKN2A R80* at 36% VAF. Those fractions, combined with four primaries before age 60 and a mother with melanoma + uterine cancer, fit the classic signal for a hereditary cancer syndrome — most likely Li-Fraumeni or the CDKN2A melanoma-pancreatic variant.

Why this matters: if she's germline-positive, her cells can't repair DNA damage as efficiently as a normal person's. Ordinary inflammatory or mechanical insults that wouldn't cause cancer in most people do in her body. This reframes the strategy: it's not about finding one carcinogen — it's about reducing every possible source of chronic cellular damage, knowing her cellular repair budget is lower than average. This is why germline testing is the single most consequential next step.

Pattern 02 · directly modifiable today
Confidence: moderate · evidence-supported, undertreated

Two chronic inflammatory conditions are doing carcinogenic work daily

Oral lichen planus since 2017 and generalized periodontitis live continuously in her mouth. Both produce the same biological cascade: T-cell-mediated inflammation → reactive oxygen species → epithelial DNA damage → constant cell turnover to replace damaged cells → more opportunities for a bad mutation to escape.

The 2023–2025 literature directly implicates Fusobacterium nucleatum and Porphyromonas gingivalis — the dominant periodontitis bacteria — as active drivers of OSCC progression, not just bystanders. And in her specific case, Dr. Kang's biopsy clinical note explicitly says "generalized periodontitis, gingival inflammation" — and the new dysplasia was found at the edge of an inflamed tooth socket. The geographic match is hard to ignore.

Where the wedge is
Aggressive periodontal control + consistent topical clobetasol for the OLP are both inexpensive, low-risk, and have data behind them. Neither appears to be on the current chart as an active priority.

What "cure" means honestly

There is no known cure for "having had multifocal oral cancer." What exists is the ability to reduce the chance of the next one by attacking every modifiable input — even small ones that wouldn't matter in a normal-risk person but matter for her.

The closest thing to a "cure" framing: (1) confirm the genetic predisposition so the strategy is calibrated to her actual repair capacity; (2) treat chronic inflammation as if it were carcinogenic — because for her it functionally is; (3) remove mechanical co-factors; (4) use the repurposed-drug stack as background risk reduction.

What this means for the family

Ch. 08 · hereditary risk for Jan's daughter and son · click each card to expand

Two of the mutations in Jan's tumor — TP53 R213P and CDKN2A R80* — have established hereditary forms. The variant fractions (34% and 36%) are consistent with — but don't prove — a germline origin. Combined with her four primaries and her mother's melanoma + uterine + cervical precancer history, the probability that at least one is inherited is meaningful. Formal germline testing on Jan is the trigger for everything below.

If TP53 R213P turns out to be germline — Li-Fraumeni syndrome Wider risk · higher intensity surveillance

Inheritance: autosomal dominant. Each of Jan's children has a 50% chance of inheriting it.

Cancer spectrum: wider than most syndromes. Lifetime cancer risk by age 70 is roughly 70% in men and ~90% in women (the female number is higher because of very high breast cancer risk in LFS).

  • Sarcomas — bone and soft-tissue, can occur in childhood and young adulthood
  • Breast cancer — extraordinarily high risk in women, often before age 40
  • Brain tumors — gliomas, choroid plexus tumors
  • Adrenocortical carcinoma — characteristic of LFS
  • Leukemias and lymphomas
  • Many other cancers at elevated rates

Surveillance if positive — the Toronto Protocol

The published screening program shown to detect tumors asymptomatically and improve survival:

  • Annual whole-body MRI starting young
  • Annual brain MRI
  • Annual breast MRI for women (mammography avoided to limit radiation)
  • Abdominal/pelvic ultrasound every 3–4 months
  • Comprehensive labs every 6 months
The radiation point
LFS carriers should minimize medical radiation exposure for life. MRI and ultrasound replace CT and mammography wherever possible. This is one of the more practical differences carriers live with — and one of the easiest to get right with informed providers.
If CDKN2A R80* turns out to be germline — melanoma-pancreatic syndrome Narrower risk · cleaner protocol · best fit for Jan's mother's history

Inheritance: autosomal dominant. Each child has a 50% chance of inheriting it. This one is particularly suggestive because Jan's mother had melanoma — a perfect fit for this syndrome.

Cancer spectrum:

  • Melanoma — 28–67% lifetime risk depending on family pattern, often multiple melanomas
  • Pancreatic cancer — 5–24% lifetime risk
  • Less commonly: head/neck SCC, lung cancer

Surveillance if positive — the CAPS Consortium protocol

  • Annual full-skin dermatology exam starting in young adulthood
  • Annual pancreatic surveillance starting around age 40 — alternating endoscopic ultrasound and MRI/MRCP
  • Aggressive sun protection — daily SPF 30+, hats, sun-protective clothing
  • No tanning beds, ever

This syndrome is the more clinically "manageable" of the two if positive — both melanoma and pancreatic cancer benefit substantially from early detection. Routine dermatology surveillance catches melanomas at curable stages in nearly all carriers who stay on protocol.

What both kids should do now, regardless of Jan's test result Low-cost, low-burden, high-value
  1. Document the family history carefully. Jan's four primaries with dates, her mother's three cancers, ages of onset, and any other relatives with cancer. Bring this to any new doctor — don't bury it in a new-patient form.
  2. Each get established with a primary care doctor who actually knows the full story, not just sees it on a chart.
  3. Annual full-skin dermatology exam. Even without CDKN2A confirmation, Jan's mother had melanoma. This is low-cost, often covered by insurance as preventive care.
  4. Avoid elective CT scans where MRI or ultrasound would work. This is the easiest hedge in case LFS turns out to be the diagnosis.
  5. Bank a saliva or blood sample. Some genetics services (Color, Invitae) let you submit a sample now and pay for testing later — useful for cascade testing once Jan's results are in.
  6. Hold off on broad genetic testing for the kids until Jan's result is in. Testing the kids first is more expensive (full panel vs. single-variant cascade test) and harder to interpret without knowing the family variant.
After Jan's germline test — what each child does Decision tree, expanded

If Jan's result is positive for either gene

  • Both kids pursue cascade genetic testing — testing only for the specific known family variant. Cheap (~$100–300), often covered by insurance with a known variant on file.
  • If a child tests positive, they enter the relevant surveillance protocol (Toronto for TP53, CAPS for CDKN2A).
  • If a child tests negative, they're back to general-population risk for that gene. The family history still informs some routine surveillance (skin checks especially), but the intensive protocols don't apply.

If Jan's result is negative for both targeted variants — but she also did a broad multi-gene panel

  • Lower urgency for the kids. But the family pattern (Jan's 4 primaries + her mother's 3 cancers) still suggests a hereditary contribution somewhere.
  • A genetics counselor can advise whether a broader panel for the kids makes sense, or whether the conclusion is "no identifiable hereditary variant — proceed with family-history-informed routine care."

If broad panel was not done — or comes back uninformative

  • Pattern still warrants baseline awareness. Each kid should at minimum maintain annual dermatology and have their PCP keep the family history visible.

One important thing to hold onto Perspective

This is information, not a sentence. If either syndrome turns out to be present in either child, the answer is intensive surveillance — and the data on that is good.
