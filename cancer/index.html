What "moderate dysplasia" means

Oral epithelial dysplasia is a spectrum: mild → moderate → severe → carcinoma in situ → invasive SCC. Moderate is the middle of that range and is treated as high-grade under the WHO binary system. Reported rates of malignant transformation vary widely with patient context: low-risk patients see ~10% transformation over years; in a patient with prior multifocal SCC + lichen planus background + post-radiation field, the figure is substantially higher.

Typical management in this setting

In a high-risk patient the standard approach is surgical excision with negative margins, often as a small in-office or outpatient procedure. Close follow-up replaces routine surveillance. The presence of generalized periodontitis means infection control / oral hygiene optimization runs in parallel — and the lesion's location in a previously irradiated field requires the surgeon to factor osteoradionecrosis risk (PENTO consideration) into the plan.

Pattern 01 · now the leading unifying explanation
Confidence: strong · pending germline test

An inherited DNA-repair vulnerability — most likely germline CDKN2A — appears to be the engine

Her tumor showed TP53 R213P at 34% VAF and CDKN2A R80* at 36% VAF. Both fractions are consistent with germline inheritance. Combined with four primaries before age 60 and her mother's melanoma + uterine cancer, this fits the classic signal for hereditary cancer — most likely the CDKN2A melanoma-pancreatic-HNSCC variant, possibly with concurrent Li-Fraumeni involvement.

The phenotype match that changes the framing

Jeong et al. 2022 (J Otolaryngol Head Neck Surg, PMC8818223) reported a 39-year-old never-smoker female with multifocal recurrent oral SCC harboring germline CDKN2A G101W. Her mother had melanoma and tongue cancer. The authors proposed routine germline CDKN2A testing in familial / young HNSCC and HNSCC-spectrum surveillance in carriers. Jan's clinical pattern is a near-perfect match. If her CDKN2A R80* is germline, the entire framework collapses into a clean Knudson two-hit FAMMM-with-HNSCC-spectrum picture — a familial syndrome that explains everything without invoking other drivers.

Two important caveats from the deeper literature:

  • "R213P" is an unusual TP53 nucleotide notation. The much more commonly reported R213 variants are R213* (a well-described pathogenic LFS nonsense allele), R213Q, R213K. R213P is rare in IARC/ClinVar. Worth asking the genetic counselor to confirm the exact nucleotide change (likely c.638G>C) and reclassify against the IARC TP53 database — if it's actually R213*, the pathogenicity call is unambiguous.
  • TP53 VAF ~34% in tumor could be germline, postzygotic mosaic, or clonal-hematopoiesis contamination. Discrimination requires testing both blood AND cultured skin fibroblasts. Identical TP53 in multiple of her prior tumors + low in blood + present in fibroblasts → mosaic LFS, which is well-recognized and would also explain multifocality.

Why this matters: if she's germline-positive, her cells can't repair DNA damage as efficiently as a normal person's. Ordinary inflammatory or mechanical insults that wouldn't cause cancer in most people do in her body. This reframes the strategy: it's not about finding one carcinogen — it's about reducing every possible source of chronic cellular damage, knowing her repair budget is lower than average. Germline testing remains the single most consequential next step.

Does Jan have PMOL?

The 2021 Woo/Thompson expert consensus on PVL/PMOL requires: (a) multifocal disease (≥2 separate sites), (b) progression over time (new sites, expansion, or histologic worsening), and (c) exclusion of clear etiology like heavy tobacco use. What AHNS 2024 actually recommends

The American Head and Neck Society's Cancer Prevention Service published an updated 2024 guideline (Gates et al., Head & Neck, Nov 2024 — the most current US expert consensus on this exact problem).

  • Formal excision or ablation for any dysplastic lesion (mild, moderate, or severe) when feasible.
  • Excisional margins: 2–5 mm in depth and width.
  • Clinical surveillance every 3 months for moderate dysplasia.
  • Re-biopsy every 12–18 months for moderate dysplasia even if visually stable.
  • Active surveillance for at least 5 years — transformation risk persists 10–15 years.
  • No single chemopreventive agent has durable efficacy as monotherapy.
